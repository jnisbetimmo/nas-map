🇬🇧 UK Anti-Money Laundering

Legislative Framework

Estate agents acting as broker agents for foreign properties are subject to the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017) and the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA). HMRC is the supervisory authority for estate agencies. As of 2025, HM Treasury has published draft amendments introducing a more risk-based, proportionate approach.

Key Obligations

  • Register with HMRC for AML supervision before commencing activity
  • Appoint a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO)
  • Conduct Customer Due Diligence (CDD) on all prospective buyers/sellers — including identity verification, source of funds, and beneficial ownership
  • Apply Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) for high-risk clients, Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), and cross-border transactions
  • File Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) to the National Crime Agency (NCA) where money laundering is suspected
  • Maintain records of all CDD for a minimum of 5 years
  • The 2025 NRA specifically flags real estate as a high-risk sector for opaque ownership structures
  • Digital ID verification frameworks are being formalised — all digital tools used must meet forthcoming HMRC/government criteria

🇪🇸 Spanish Anti-Money Laundering

Legislative Framework

Ley 10/2010, de 28 de abril (as amended by Royal Decree-Law 7/2021 transposing the 5th EU AMLD) and Royal Decree 304/2014 govern AML obligations. The supervisory authority is SEPBLAC (Servicio Ejecutivo de la Comisión de Prevención del Blanqueo de Capitales e Infracciones Monetarias). Real estate intermediaries are expressly listed as "obligated subjects" under Article 2.1.

Key Obligations

  • Real estate agents carrying out intermediation are obligated subjects — including those acting on behalf of a foreign principal
  • Mandatory KYC: identify and verify customers and beneficial owners before establishing a business relationship
  • Verify the source and legitimacy of funds used in any property transaction
  • Apply Enhanced Due Diligence for cross-border clients and transactions above risk thresholds
  • Establish an Internal Control Body (OCI) responsible for AML procedures
  • File Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) with SEPBLAC
  • Annual AML training for all staff involved in regulated activity
  • Criminal penalties: up to 6 years imprisonment + fines of up to 3× the asset value for money laundering offences

🇬🇧 UK Data Protection

Legislative Framework

The UK GDPR (retained EU law, post-Brexit) and Data Protection Act 2018 apply. The ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) is the supervisory authority. The EU has formally renewed its adequacy decision for the UK on 19 December 2025, allowing free data flows from EEA to UK.

Key Obligations for Cross-Border Transfer

  • The UK is adequate under EU GDPR — data can flow freely from Spain to the UK
  • The UK must recognise EEA (including Spain) as adequate — data flows freely in both directions
  • A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) should be in place between the UK broker agent and the Spanish principal company
  • Clients must be informed of the identity of both data controllers and the purpose of transfer
  • Lawful basis for AML/KYC data processing: legal obligation (Art. 6(1)(c) UK GDPR) — consent is not required for compliance purposes
  • Data subjects have rights of access, rectification, erasure (where applicable), and portability
  • Privacy notices must be provided at point of collection

🇪🇸 Spanish / EU Data Protection

Legislative Framework

EU GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) and Organic Law 3/2018 (LOPDGDD) apply. The AEPD (Agencia Española de Protección de Datos) is the supervisory authority. Minimum age of consent in Spain is 16 years under LOPDGDD.

Key Obligations for Cross-Border Transfer

  • UK has adequacy status under EU GDPR (renewed December 2025) — transfer from Spain to UK is lawful without additional safeguards
  • For AML/CDD data processing, Article 6(1)(c) GDPR provides lawful basis — no separate consent required (confirmed by Royal Decree-Law 7/2021)
  • A formal Data Processing Agreement between the two entities is strongly recommended and best practice
  • Clients must receive a clear privacy notice (Información Básica sobre Protección de Datos) per LOPDGDD
  • AEPD must be notified of the cross-border data flow arrangement in the Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)
  • Non-compliance: fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher)

⚡ Regulatory Action Points — Both Entities

01

The UK company must be registered with HMRC for AML supervision as an estate agent. Confirm registration is current and covers broker agent activity for foreign (Spanish) properties.

02

A bilateral Data Processing Agreement (DPA / Data Sharing Agreement) must be executed between the UK broker agent and the Spanish principal company, defining controller/processor roles, data categories, retention periods, and security measures.

03

Both entities must maintain a Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) documenting the cross-border data flow, purposes, legal bases, and security arrangements.

04

A joint Privacy Notice must be made available to all prospective clients before or at the point of data collection, in both English and Spanish, covering both UK GDPR and EU GDPR requirements.

05

The UK entity must appoint or designate an MLRO. The Spanish entity must establish or confirm its OCI (Internal Control Body) per Ley 10/2010.

06

All client identity documents and KYC records must be retained for a minimum of 5 years post-transaction or post-business relationship, under both UK MLR 2017 and Spanish Ley 10/2010.

07

Any transaction where the source of funds or client identity raises suspicion must trigger a SAR (UK) or STR (Spain) filing. Neither entity may "tip off" the client that a report has been made.

08

The broker agent agreement between the two companies should explicitly allocate AML compliance responsibilities and acknowledge the cross-border data transfer arrangement, to provide legal clarity under both jurisdictions.

09
